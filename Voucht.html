The issue isn't the quality of independent businesses — it's that they're invisible. When someone searches "coffee near me" or "barber open now," Google surfaces whoever has the most reviews at the highest rating. The average top-ranked local business has 47 Google reviews. Most independents have fewer than 15.

"97% of people read reviews before choosing a local business."

BrightLocal Consumer Review Survey, 2026

The visibility gap compounds. More reviews → higher ranking → more foot traffic → more reviews. And fixing it costs money most independents don't have. Local SEO agencies charge £300–£1,500 a month. A barber running a one-chair shop in South London can't write that cheque. So nothing changes, and the gap widens.

A one-star improvement in average rating leads to a 5–9% increase in revenue — on a business doing £30k a month, that's £1,500–2,700. The stakes are real. The solution just hasn't existed at a price they can afford.

02 — The insight

"Your best
marketing team
is already in
your venue."

The problem isn't that customers won't vouch for you. It's that nobody ever asks them right. They feel good about their flat white. They'd happily leave a star. But that moment passes in seconds — they pay, they leave, the impulse is gone.

The fix isn't digital. It's physical. A pad on every table. A tap of a phone. A frictionless bridge between the moment someone feels good about their experience and the moment they'd tell Google about it. The review path was always there — it just needed a trigger that was impossible to miss.

And once you're collecting reviews, you're also collecting the data to understand exactly where you rank against every competitor on your street — and exactly what it takes to climb.

03 — What I built

Four layers.
One system.

Voucht isn't an NFC card seller. It's a managed local dominance service — four interlocking components that work together to move a business up Google's local rankings and keep them climbing.

01 — Hub page

The branded destination

A hosted page branded to each business. Google review link, socials, website, menu, events — and for Growth subscribers, the UGC content raffle. Everything a customer needs, one scan away.

The page is managed by Voucht. Updates via WhatsApp: 48-hour turnaround on Pioneer, same-day on Growth. No login. No CMS. No friction for the owner.

02 — NFC / QR pads

The physical trigger

Branded table pads installed at the venue. A customer taps their phone — or scans the QR code if NFC isn't available — and lands on the hub page in under two seconds.

Pioneer subscribers get 5 pads. Growth gets 10. Additional pads available at £6 each. Window and counter QR stickers included on both tiers. The pads remain Voucht property — returned or reprogrammed if the business cancels.

03 — Weekly competitive snapshot

The retention engine

Every Monday morning the business gets a report showing where they rank for their category in their area — their position, their review count, every nearby competitor, and how many reviews they need to overtake the next one.

Pioneer gets their own ranking. Growth gets the full competitor breakdown. This is what makes Voucht sticky — the business is watching their numbers move week over week, and they know exactly who they're racing.

04 — UGC content library + raffle

The content engine

Growth tier only. Customers snap photos or videos of their experience — food, coffee, the venue — and are entered into a weekly prize draw run by the business. The business gets a growing library of authentic, usable content without hiring a photographer or writing a brief.

The prize is typically a free drink or meal (cost: £3–5). The winner gets announced on the business's social media — which generates more reach, and brings customers back to enter again next week.

The review and raffle paths are kept entirely separate — see the design decision below.

04 — The design challenge

Two paths.
Never crossed.

This is where the product design had to do legal work. Google's review policy is explicit: reviews cannot be incentivised. If a customer leaves a review in exchange for a reward, that review can be removed and the business can face penalties. The UK Gambling Act 2005 adds a second constraint: prize draws have specific eligibility rules and must state "no purchase necessary."

Getting this wrong would have invalidated both features. The solution is structural — two completely separate paths on the hub page, designed from the start to never reference each other.

Google Review Policy

Organic only — no incentive

The review button is a direct link to Google. No form. No filter. No conditional step. A customer who wants to leave a review taps straight through. The raffle is never mentioned on that path, and a review is never mentioned on the raffle path. The separation is enforced in the UX, not just in policy text.

UK Gambling Act 2005 — Free Prize Draws

Legal without a licence — with conditions

A free prize draw doesn't require a gambling licence as long as entry is genuinely free and "no purchase necessary" is clearly stated. Voucht provides the entry mechanism; the business runs the draw and fulfils the prize. If a business wants to restrict entry to paying customers, the rules change — they're advised to keep it open.

Design maturity

Compliance baked in, not bolted on

Both constraints were known before the hub page was sketched. The two-path architecture wasn't a retroactive workaround — it's the intended structure. That distinction matters to the businesses using this system: they're protected because the legal thinking happened before the first button was placed, not after.

05 — The brand

An identity built
on the mechanism.

The brand had to work in two environments: on screen (the hub page and weekly snapshot) and in the physical world (the table pads). Bold enough to scan at a glance from across a café. Distinctive enough to own a table. Trustworthy enough to make a stranger tap.

The dark theme isn't a trend choice — it makes the green pop in any lighting condition, which matters when the pad is competing with ambient light in a busy venue.

The mark

A tall vertical stroke — the check, the promise delivered — paired with a rotated square carrying a QR finder-pattern detail at its corner. Two objects, two behaviours, one mark. The scan is embedded in the letter. Every time someone looks at the logo they're looking at the product.
