The hook

Everyone can build anything now. Almost nobody is building it to last.

Sixty-nine seconds walking through four ways to build something that dies, then one that doesn't.

The insight

We hit the moment building got easy.

Describe an app in a sentence and a machine returns a working product, front end, back end, database, the lot. The constraint that used to separate people, can you actually build it, collapsed. So the question moved: not "can you build it" but "is it worth surviving." The loudest builders answered wrong, flipping screenshots of revenue instead of making anything that lasts. Build to flip. Build to impress. Build to launch before the thing is real. The announcement became the product.

Base 44 sits right in the middle of that culture, a vibe-coding platform, plain-English prompt in, full web app out, which means it attracts exactly the screenshot-flippers. So instead of pretending the hype doesn't exist, I made an ad that's in on the joke: it names every cheap reason people build, lets them collapse on screen, then plants its flag with the ones still standing next year.

A brand confident enough to mock its own worst users earns the trust of its best ones.

The core strategy

A morality play with four sins.

Each sin is a card, "When you build to ___", and each one escalates:

SIN 01

Flip

Get-rich hysteria. A machine printing money that means nothing.

SIN 02

Impress

Spectacle with no destination. A road to nowhere.

SIN 03

Launch

The premature victory lap. A valuation ripping on a product that doesn't exist.

SIN 04

Too Fast

The inevitable crash. Launch → 404 → an explosion that ends the cycle.

Then the turn: "But when you build with…" → Base 44 deploys in a clean terminal → "You. Build. To. Last."

The retention engine underneath it is a single deliberate contrast: raw, low-res internet-culture wreckage against premium, high-resolution engineering UI. The sins are shot degraded, grain, chaos, broadcast junk; Base 44 arrives composed, exact, quiet. That texture-whiplash is what stops the thumb, the audience feels the difference before they've read a word.
